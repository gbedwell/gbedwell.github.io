HIV-1 preferrentially integrates within the bodies of highly expressed genes in gene-dense regions of chromatin. In addition, integration into certain genes has been linked to infected cell survival in patients. However, a comprehensive understanding of the gene targeting biases during HIV-1 integration remains unknown. In this paper, we address this issue by deriving phenomenological models of random genic integration. We use these models to identify recurrently integrated genes (RIGs), recurrently avoided genes (RAGs), and genes targeted at levels indistinguishable from random (referred to in the manuscript as non-outliers). We find that integration into RIGs accounts for nearly 70% of all genic integration events both <i>in vitro</i> and <i>in vivo</i>, despite RIGs accounting for just ~13% of all human genes. In addition, a comprehensive comparison of genic targeting patterns <i>in vitro</i> and <i>in vivo</i> highlights that integration into only a small number of genes (6-7 by our estimation) confer infected cell survival in patients.
