HIV-1 preferrentially integrates within the bodies of highly expressed genes in gene-dense regions of chromatin. In addition, integration into certain genes has been linked to infected cell survival in patients. However, a comprehensive understanding of gene targeting biases during HIV-1 integration remains absent. In this paper, we address this issue by deriving phenomenological models of random genic integration. We use these models to identify recurrently integrated genes (RIGs), recurrently avoided genes (RAGs), and genes targeted at levels indistinguishable from random (referred to in the manuscript as non-outliers). We find that integration into RIGs accounts for nearly 70% of all genic integration events both <i>in vitro</i> and <i>in vivo</i>, despite RIGs accounting for just a fraction of all human genes. We additionally show that the spatial localization of genic integration site selection is well-conserved between datasets dervied from <i>in vitro</i> infections, untreated HIV-1 infected patients, and HIV-1 infected patients on suppressive antiretroviral therapy (ART). Finally, we systematically assess both untreated and ART-treated patient-derived datasets for genes showing the hallmarks of integration-linked infected cell survival. Strikingly, our analysis identified just 6 genes with these characteristics. Our results strongly suggest that integration itself is only a very minor player in the establishment of the HIV-1 latent reservoir.
